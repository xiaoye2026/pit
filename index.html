[Vol. 1, No. 1] Long-Term Stability of Biodegradable Plastic Bags in Synthetic Immortality Buffers  -  L. Chemist, Q. Polymer & Z. Optimist  (2026)
Abstract.
In this study we investigate the degradation behavior of commercially branded “100% biodegradable” plastic bags when immersed for 50 years of simulated time in a laboratory-designed immortality buffer—an aqueous solution specifically tailored to prevent every known degradation pathway. Our accelerated aging data reveal that, under sufficiently protected conditions, biodegradable plastics remain impressively non-biodegradable. The results provide no practical benefit for environmental policy, but substantially improve our understanding of how to keep useless samples intact for future useless studies.
[Vol. 1, No. 1] Closed-Loop Air Pollution: A Pilot Study on Capturing Industrial Emissions and Releasing Them Somewhere Else  -  H. Enviro, R. Stack & C. Relocator  (2026)
Abstract.
We propose and experimentally evaluate a “closed-loop” approach to air pollution control in which particulate-laden flue gas is captured, compressed, transported, and re-released at locations with limited political representation and no monitoring stations. Using a bench-scale stack and a set of strategically uncalibrated sensors, we demonstrate that local air quality indicators can be dramatically improved near the original source without any measurable reduction in total emissions. The concept is found to be technologically feasible, ethically indefensible, and therefore highly aligned with historical practices in environmental mismanagement.
[Vol. 1, No. 1] Finite-Element Modeling of Coffee Stains on Laboratory Notebooks as a Proxy for Concrete Cracking  -  M. Materials & Y. Overfitter  (2026)
Abstract.
Materials engineers routinely use complex models to mimic the cracking of cementitious composites, while routinely spilling coffee on the same notebooks that host their calculations. Here we conduct a detailed finite-element simulation of ring-shaped coffee stains on laboratory paper and attempt to correlate the stain morphology with hypothetical micro-crack propagation in concrete. The model fits the stains almost perfectly, yet fails to predict any property of real construction materials. We conclude that the strongest correlation observed is between grant funding and one’s ability to justify arbitrary simulations.
